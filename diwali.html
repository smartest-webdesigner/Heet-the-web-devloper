Diwali

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is one of the most important and widely celebrated Hindu festivals symbolizing the victory of light over darkness and good over evil. Significance and Meaning Diwali marks the return of Lord Rama to his kingdom Ayodhya after 14 years of exile and his victory over the demon king Ravana, according to the epic Ramayana. It is celebrated to honor the triumph of righteousness, prosperity, and new beginnings. How Diwali is Celebrated Lighting Lamps: Homes, temples, and streets are decorated with traditional oil lamps called diyas and colorful electric lights to represent light overcoming darkness. Fireworks: The festival features displays of fireworks and crackers, symbolizing joy and celebration. Rangoli: Intricate and colorful patterns called rangoli are created on floors near entrances to welcome guests and deities. Prayer and Worship: Devotees perform pujas (prayers) to Goddess Lakshmi, the deity of wealth and prosperity, and Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles. Festive Meals and Sweets: Families prepare and share special dishes and sweets like ladoos, barfis, and other traditional delicacies. Exchange of Gifts: It is customary to exchange gifts and sweets among family members, friends, and neighbors. Regional Variations While Diwali is celebrated across India, regions have their unique traditions: In Maharashtra, including Pune, people especially celebrate Lakshmi Puja on the main day, marking a night of worship, prayers, and lighting of lamps.
